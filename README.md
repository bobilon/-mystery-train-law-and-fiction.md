# Paraphrasis: Jeremy Bentham's Impossible-Yet-Indispensable Thought Technology for Reconciling Law & Fiction — aka You Gotta Know Shit from Shinola

*On the road to the analog-to-digital Y2K split and beyond. This is the tool strand: it forges the instrument the other tbd strands use. English Language as technology — and the legal fiction as the oldest software running on it.*

---

Start with the cadaver in the jury box everybody ignores like Banksy's painted pink elephant in the room, present but not voting, that every juror sees but won't admit it out of fear that the Judge or their fellow jurors will have them locked up for contempt. You don't get it? Let's reboot.

At University College London, in a glass case in the Student Centre, sits the preserved corpse of Jeremy Bentham — his actual skeleton, dressed in his own clothes, topped with a wax head, wheeled out on occasion and recorded in the minutes, the story goes, as "present but not voting." He named it the Auto-Icon, and its production, construction, and post-mortem display would be effected by performative force — words with the power to effect action in the real, like a Judge "ordering" someone held without bail, after which that person is returned to jail by the force of the words (what philosophers call illocutionary and perlocutionary force).

Bentham designed it himself, writing it into his last will & testament, and in doing so in his capacity as UCL's spiritual founder, University College has agreed for nearly two centuries to treat a dressed skeleton as a present member of its community.

Hold that image, because it is this strand's point in its entirety made literal. A man who spent his life attacking *fictions* — propositions treated as true that everyone knows are not — became, by his own design, the purest fiction the institution owns: a corpse the university agrees to treat as alive, because the university's identity depends on treating it so. The auto-icon is not a curiosity. It is the working model. Everything in this strand is a version of the dressed skeleton: an impossible thing the system agrees to treat as real, because the value depends on the agreement.

UCL is the right room for it for a second reason. Founded in 1826 as the "godless college of Gower Street," it was built to be the structural opposite of the gatekeeping institution — open to the nonconformists, Jews, Catholics, and women that Oxford and Cambridge locked out by religious test. And it was caught in exactly the trap this strand exists to expose: the establishment let it teach but withheld, for a decade, the legal right to call itself a "university" and to grant degrees — because the *name* was the contested thing, not the teaching. The same trap, almost to the letter, that starved Newman's locked-out Catholic University in Dublin a few decades later. Two institutions built by the excluded; both denied not the right to instruct but the right to *confer* — to issue the credential, which is to say, to operate the fiction. The credential is a fictitious entity. Withholding it was withholding the fiction's legal force. The whole fight was over who gets to run the machine that turns language into authority.

That machine is the subject. Bentham gave us the manual.

## What Bentham actually said

The colloquial "legal fiction" — the law pretending something false is true, "the corporation is a person," "the finding relates back" — is the shallow version. Bentham's actual theory is deeper and far more useful, and it was a theory of *language*, which is why it belongs at the front of a series whose masthead reads English Language.

Bentham drew a distinction most people collapse: between a *fiction* and the *name of a fictitious entity*. A fictitious entity is anything that has no physical existence — that you cannot point to, weigh, or stub your toe on — but that language must posit in order to function. A right. An obligation. A power. A duty. A corporation. An estate. A title. None of these exist in the way a table or a wafer of silicon exists. They exist, in Bentham's exact and famous formulation, in language alone: "To language, then — to language alone — it is that fictitious entities owe their existence; their impossible, yet indispensable existence."

Read that sentence slowly, because it is the keystone of everything downstream. *Impossible* — these things are not real in the way physical objects are real. *Yet indispensable* — you cannot have law, commerce, property, or thought without them. The fictitious entity is a piece of linguistic technology: a word that does engineering work, that moves value and confers authority and binds parties, despite having no referent you can touch. Language is the substrate; the fiction is the program that runs on it. This is the masthead claim — English Language as technology — stated at its root. The legal fiction is the oldest software, and it has been running since the first time someone said "this is mine" and another person agreed to be bound by the sentence.

Bentham was not against fictitious entities. You cannot be; they are the air law breathes. He was against their *fraudulent use* — against the fiction deployed in deceptive or fallacious argument, the dressed skeleton wheeled in to win a vote it has no business voting in. And he gave a tool for telling the legitimate fiction from the fraudulent one. He called it *paraphrasis*: take the fictitious entity and translate it back into real entities — into the actual physical things and actual human actions it stands for — and see whether the translation holds, whether meaning and truth survive the exchange. A legitimate fiction paraphrases cleanly: "my right to this field" cashes out into real facts about who may walk where and who may stop whom. A fraudulent fiction does not paraphrase — it dissolves, or it reveals that the real entities underneath do not support the authority the word claims.

Paraphrasis is the grocer's grammar. It is the same instrument, two hundred years apart. The grocer's son who knows what things actually cost is performing paraphrasis on the price: taking the fictitious entity — the seventeen-million-dollar brand-asset, the edition-of-twenty-five, the corporation that owns the painting — and translating it back into real entities: a stencil on canvas that cost a substrate and a printer, a head-count of partners who held positions when a round closed, a shell registered to an address. The translation either holds or it doesn't. When it doesn't, you have found a fraudulent fiction wearing the clothes of a legitimate one. Bentham named the method. The grocer's grammar runs it on the art market.

One more detail, because it seals the strand to the masthead. Bentham's theory of fictions was not published in his lifetime. It sat scattered through his manuscripts until C. K. Ogden — a figure of the Vienna Circle and the founder of modern semiotics, the man who would later translate Vaihinger's *Philosophy of As If* — gathered it in the early 1930s and put it on the map. Ogden's own claim was that the great defect of the continental fiction-theorists was their failure to stress *the linguistic factor* — a step, he said, that Bentham had taken a century earlier. So the lineage runs: a utilitarian's buried theory of how language manufactures authority, recovered by a semiotician, landing in the twentieth century exactly when the disciplines that should have used it — economics, law, art history — were busy building equilibrium models and connoisseurship catalogs that had no slot for it. The tool existed. It was filed in the wrong drawer. This series is, in part, the act of taking it out.

## The fiction = the IP right = the author-function = the manufactured edition

Here is the move that makes Bentham load-bearing for the whole braid, and it is offered as the analytical frame it is — not as intellectual history. Bentham did not write about modern intellectual property; copyright as we know it postdates his fiction work, and the corporate-personhood fiction he attacked is adjacent to, not identical with, the edition-scarcity that concerns the art strand. The claim is structural, and it is this: **the legal fiction, the intellectual-property right, the author-function, and the manufactured art edition are all the same kind of object — fictitious entities that owe their existence to language alone, and that have real economic force precisely because everyone agrees to treat the constructed thing as real.**

Watch them line up.

The *legal fiction* — "this corporation is a person," "this entity owns this asset" — is a proposition with no physical referent that the system enforces as true because the system's operation depends on it.

The *intellectual-property right* — "this person holds the exclusive right to reproduce this arrangement of words or marks" — is identical in structure. There is no physical thing that *is* the copyright; there is a socially enforced sentence about who may copy what. It paraphrases into real entities (the author, the text, the printing, the act of copying) and the law enforces the paraphrase. A legitimate IP right cashes out cleanly. The interesting cases are the ones that don't.

The *author-function* — the twentieth-century recognition (Foucault's term, but the insight is older and Bentham is one of its grandfathers) that "the author" is not simply the person who wrote the words but a *constructed entity* that organizes how a body of work is read, valued, and owned — is the same fictitious entity again, seen from the literary side. "Banksy" is an author-function: a name that confers value and organizes a market, owing its existence to language and agreement, not to a single locatable human hand. The signature on the back of the canvas is the fiction's instrument — the linguistic act that enrolls the object into the oeuvre. When the artist signs a stated edition of fifteen with the impossible notation 16/15, she is using the syntax of the fiction to break the fiction — performing paraphrasis on her own authorship in the only language the market reads.

And the *manufactured edition* — "this print is number 10 of 25, struck in 2003" — is the purest commercial fiction of the set. The edition number is a fictitious entity in Bentham's exact sense: it has no physical existence (nothing about the object itself makes it the tenth of twenty-five; the number is a sentence written on a stretcher), it owes its existence entirely to language and agreement, and it is indispensable to the value because the scarcity it asserts is the entire basis of the price. Run paraphrasis on it. Translate "edition of 25, 2003, acquired through Lazarides Gallery" back into real entities, and you find a gallery that was not incorporated until 2004, a publisher and an authentication body that did not exist until later still, and a "head-count" that maps onto partnership positions rather than a contemporaneous artist's run. The fiction does not paraphrase. The translation fails. You have found the dressed skeleton.

This is why Law & Fiction is the tool strand. It supplies the single instrument the others use. Education is the history of one fictitious entity — the credential, the degree, the name "University" — and the fight over who may issue it. Letters–Media is the history of the IP fiction learning to sell the reproduction rather than the original. Industry is the history of the etched mark migrating from the printer's plate to the silicon wafer, carrying its fictions with it. Economy–Markets is the history of fictitious entities — the corporation, the edition, the brand — being priced as if they were real entities, until the paraphrase fails in public. Every strand is a strand of fictitious entities. Bentham gave us the way to read them: translate back to the real, and watch whether the sentence survives.

## Why the tool matters now

A fiction holds as long as the agreement holds, and the agreement holds as long as nobody runs the paraphrase out loud. For most of the analog era, the institutions that *could* have run it — the courts, the academy, the press, the auction houses — did not, either because they were party to the fiction or because the framework for reading it had been filed in the wrong drawer. The tool sat in Ogden's edition, in the philosophy stacks, while the economics departments ran models with no term for the impossible-yet-indispensable and the art market ran on connoisseurship that took the edition number at its word.

The digital migration changes the standing of the fiction in a specific way, and it is the way the whole series turns on. A fictitious entity owes its existence to language *and to the scarcity of its enforcement*. In the analog world, the physical object — the signed canvas, the sealed credential, the printed and bounded book, the original plate — was the thing that made the fiction *expensive to forge and easy to authenticate*, which is what let the fiction carry value. Digital reproduction removes that. When the substrate is infinitely copyable, the fiction can no longer hide behind the scarcity of the object; it stands exposed as what it always was — a sentence the system agreed to treat as true. The auto-icon works because there is one skeleton in one case. The manufactured edition works because there are, supposedly, only twenty-five. Remove the physical scarcity and the fiction has to be defended on its own terms, as language, in the open — which is precisely where paraphrasis can reach it.

That is the tool, and that is why it goes first. The grocer's grammar is Benthamite paraphrasis run on a civilization's fictions at the moment the digital migration is stripping them of the physical scarcity that protected them. The facts have owners. The fictions have language. And language, at last, can be read back to the real.
